% -------------------------------------------------------
% Abstract
% -------------------------------------------------------
\def\myabstract{%
  \mdseries
This study presents a Monte Carlo simulation framework to evaluate the
performance of a \textbf{2-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)} for binary
intent classification under simulated Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ)
conditions. Using a focused subset of the
{\textbf{Tanaos synthetic intent classifier v1}} dataset \cite{b_tanaos_dataset},
the task is restricted to \textbf{``Greeting''} and \textbf{``Farewell''} categories to
suit the 2-qubit architecture. Text utterances are vectorized via TF-IDF and
reduced to a 2-dimensional feature space using Principal Component Analysis (PCA).
The model architecture uses a ZZFeatureMap with one repetition for quantum state
encoding and a RealAmplitudes variational circuit with two repetitions, resulting
in six trainable parameters. To mitigate simulated depolarizing-noise effects, we
apply \textbf{Zero Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)} as an error mitigation strategy. The
pipeline is evaluated across discrete depolarizing noise levels of 0\%, 1\%, 3\%,
5\%, 7\%, and 10\%, together with measurement shot counts of 100, 1{,}000, and
10{,}000. For each noise-shot configuration, the experiment performs 1{,}000
seed-varied Monte Carlo trials to estimate mean accuracy and statistical variance
under finite-shot and depolarizing noise conditions.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Research in Natural Language Processing (NLP) increasingly incorporates
quantum-enhanced machine learning methods. However, current
Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices remain sensitive to
decoherence and gate errors, limiting their reliability in practical
computational settings \cite{b_phukan2025}.

NISQ-era quantum computers face two main technical constraints:
environmental decoherence and imperfect gate operations. Depending on the
hardware platform and experimental setup, gate error rates are often reported
in the 0.1\% to 1\% range. Unlike classical computational noise, quantum noise
compounds with each physical operation; as circuit depth and gate count
increase, the fidelity of the overall quantum state can decay. This operational
decay often restricts reliable computation to shallow circuit architectures
\cite{b_zhu2026}. Without error mitigation strategies, scaling current quantum
hardware beyond these limits remains constrained.

Optimizing high-dimensional Natural Language Understanding (NLU) models also
introduces the challenge of barren plateaus. In variational quantum algorithms,
barren plateaus occur when cost-function gradients vanish exponentially as the
qubit count increases, making optimization increasingly difficult
\cite{b_phukan2025}. These plateaus can trap optimizers in suboptimal search
regions, leading to premature convergence and poor classification performance
\cite{b_lung2026}.

Previous analyses of quantum noise dynamics have shown that controlled
stochastic effects can behave like a regularizer, reducing both the model's
Lipschitz constant and the Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM)
\cite{b_zhu2026}. These effects can encourage flatter minima in the loss
landscape, which have been associated with tighter Probably Approximately
Correct (PAC)-Bayesian generalization bounds \cite{b_xhelollari}.

However, this benefit introduces a critical tension between regularizing the
model and maintaining its trainability \cite{b_zhu2026, b_cincio2021}. If noise
is pushed beyond a critical threshold, state-space contraction can trigger
noise-induced barren plateaus (NIBPs), where gradients vanish exponentially and
beneficial stochastic fluctuations become disruptive instead of aiding
optimization \cite{b_xhelollari, b_higgs}. The model's response to noise also
depends on initialization: noise may assist under-optimized models while
disrupting well-converged ones, establishing a negative correlation with
baseline performance \cite{b_zhu2026}.

This study uses a Monte Carlo simulation framework to examine how simulated
depolarizing noise, measurement shot count, and Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)
affect the behavior of a fixed two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC).
The pipeline evaluates 1{,}000 seed-varied Monte Carlo trials for each discrete
configuration of depolarizing noise at 0\%, 1\%, 3\%, 5\%, 7\%, and 10\%,
combined with measurement shot counts of 100, 1{,}000, and 10{,}000. This
repeated evaluation is intended to estimate the statistical variance introduced
by simulator stochasticity and finite-shot sampling noise, both of which can
affect the measured classifier output.

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:related_literature}

\subsection{The Convergence of Quantum Computing and NLP}

The convergence of quantum computing and Natural Language Processing (NLP) represents a fundamental paradigm shift 
from purely statistical, associative methods to structure-preserving, 
categorical representations of meaning \cite{b_phukan2025, b_pal2025}.
This section focuses on the theoretical foundations of this convergence, 
specifically the "Quantum Native" hypothesis and the principle of compositionality.

\subsubsection{The "Quantum Native" Hypothesis}

The foundational motivation for Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP) is the proposition that 
human language is "quantum native" \cite{b_phukan2025, b_pal2025, b_coecke2020}.
The 'Quantum Native' hypothesis suggests that natural language fits naturally into quantum 
computers because the mathematical structures of grammar match those of quantum mechanics. 
Both frameworks use vector spaces to represent individual elements and tensor products to 
calculate how these elements interact.

This shared mathematical framework allows for a direct and systematic translation of a sentence’s 
grammatical string diagram into a quantum circuit, where words act as quantum states and 
grammatical dependencies are represented as entangling operations \cite{b_pal2025, b_coecke2020}.
Because language and quantum mechanics share this underlying categorical logic, quantum computers
are viewed as the "native hardware" for executing linguistic models.

A key advantage of this approach in the current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era is 
that linguistic structure is essentially provided as a "free lunch," meaning that the grammatical rules of 
a sentence are encoded as the hardware's physical configuration rather than being learned from data \cite{b_coecke2020}.
While classical systems view the encoding of complex grammar as exponentially expensive, the variational 
quantum circuit paradigm allows syntactic structure to be provided for "free" by defining the quantum circuit skeleton 
before training begins \cite{b_phukan2025, b_coecke2020}.

In classical models, such as Large Language Models (LLMs), grammatical rules must be learned from scratch through 
massive parameter budgets, often resulting in "black box" systems whose internal reasoning is opaque and lacks structural 
interpretability \cite{b_phukan2025, b_krawchuk2025}.

To expound on this empirical evidence, the QSANN 
(Quantum Self-Attention Neural Network) model serves as a primary 
example of this efficiency, particularly in text classification tasks.
On sentiment classification benchmarks using real-world data like the Yelp dataset, 
the QSANN was shown to outperform comparable classical self-attention baselines while using only 
49 quantum parameters against the 785 classical parameters required by the classical model—a reduction of >90\% \cite{b_phukan2025}.

This drastic reduction is rooted in the exponential representational capacity of quantum systems. 
Theoretically, an $n$-qubit system can represent $2^{n}$ states in parallel, allowing quantum 
models to inhabit high-dimensional Hilbert spaces that are exponentially larger 
than the latent spaces accessible to classical models of equivalent size. \cite{b_zengcoecke2016, b_pal2025}

\subsection{Compositionality and the DisCoCat Framework}
The theoretical cornerstone of modern Quantum Natural Language 
Processing (QNLP) is the Distributional Compositional Categorical (DisCoCat) framework \cite{b_yeung2021}. 
This model provides a mathematically rigorous unification of two central pillars of
linguistic theory: the distributional hypothesis, which posits that a word’s meaning is
determined by its context, and the principle of compositionality, which states that the meaning of a whole 
expression is a function of the meanings of its parts and the rules used to combine them \cite{b_coeckesadrzadeh2010}.

\subsubsection{Mathematical Mechanics of DisCoCat}
The DisCoCat (Distributional Compositional Categorical) framework operates by 
establishing a formal, structure-preserving mapping between formal grammar and vector space semantics \cite{b_yeung2021, b_murauer2023}.
This unification allows the model to leverage the quantitative strengths of the 
distributional hypothesis — where word meaning is derived from context, and the qualitative rigor 
of the principle of compositionality, which dictates how those meanings combine according to grammatical rules \cite{b_murauer2023, b_khatri2022}.

\subsubsection{Pregroup Grammar}
On the syntactic side, the DisCoCat framework primarily utilizes pregroup grammars, 
an algebraic approach introduced by Joachim Lambek where words are assigned abstract types.
A pregroup is a partially ordered monoid where every element $p$ has a left adjoint $p^l$
and a right adjoint $p^r$. These adjoints act as directional inverses for grammatical dependencies \cite{b_yeung2021, b_coeckesadrzadeh2010, b_khatri2022}.
For instance:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{noun} is assigned a basic type $n$.
  \item A \textbf{transitive verb} is assigned a compound type $n^r s n^l$, indicating it requires a subject ($n$) on its left and an object ($n$) on its right to form a well-typed sentence of type $s$.
  \item Linguistic reductions, such as $n \cdot n^r \rightarrow 1$, are represented as ``cups'' in a string diagram, signaling the fulfillment of these grammatical requirements.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hilbert Space Representations}
Under this framework, word meanings are represented as vectors or higher-order tensors within high-dimensional Hilbert spaces.
Words with basic types are represented as vectors, while functional words like verbs are represented
as tensors whose order is determined by their compound types \cite{b_yeung2021, b_murauer2023, b_toumi2025}. Consequently, the process of parsing a sentence 
becomes a physical process of composing these tensors \cite{b_lorenz2023, b_guarasci2022}.
This "quantum-native" structure allows the grammatical derivation of a sentence to
dictate the exact wiring of a quantum circuit, where word meanings are instantiated as parameterized quantum operations.

\subsection{Dynamics and Duality of Quantum Noise in NISQ Systems}
The utility of Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices is fundamentally governed by
the interplay between hardware-induced disturbances and the model's Hilbert Space. Unlike
classical noise, quantum noise is categorized by complex decoherence mechanisms primarily
\textbf{energy relaxation ($T_1$)} and \textbf{dephasing ($T_2$)} which manifest at the
algorithmic level as depolarizing, amplitude damping, or bit-flip error channels \cite{b_zhu2026, b_yi2025}.

\subsubsection{Principles and Mechanics of Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)}
To mitigate the systematic biases introduced by hardware decoherence, Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) 
has emerged as a premier hardware-agnostic post-processing strategy for the NISQ era \cite{b_wang2021, b_chu2026, b_njiki2026}.
ZNE operates on the core principle that if the expectation value of a physical 
observable varies smoothly with the system's noise rate, one can estimate the ideal 
"zero-noise" result by evaluating the circuit at multiple elevated noise levels and extrapolating 
the results back to the noise-free limit \cite{b_russo, b_sayapin2025, b_pennylane_zne}.
The workflow generally consists of two primary stages: noise amplification and extrapolation.

\paragraph{Controlled Noise Amplification}

In the first stage, the effective noise strength ($\lambda$) is increased in a 
controlled manner without altering the logical state of the computation. 
Several methodologies exist for scaling noise:


\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Digital Unitary Folding}: This gate-level technique replaces a unitary $U$ with the logically equivalent 
  sequence $U(U^\dagger U)^n$. This increases the circuit depth and time-to-solution, 
  effectively scaling the cumulative noise by odd factors of $s = 2n + 1$. \cite{b_sayapin2025, b_koenig2024}.
  \item \textbf{Analog Pulse Stretching:} This approach involves physically
  stretching the duration of microwave control pulses while
  inversely scaling their amplitude, exposing qubits to
  environmental decoherence mechanisms ($T_1$ and $T_2$) for
  longer durations. \cite{b_russo, b_koenig2024, b_liu2026}
  \item \textbf{Probabilistic Error Amplification (PEA)}: A model-based approach where a twirled noise model is learned
  for each circuit layer and then amplified by inserting random Pauli gates\cite{b_koenig2024}.
  \item \textbf{Cyclic Layout Permutations (CLP-ZNE)}: This "gray-box" method exploits the spatial 
  non-uniformity of gate fidelities on a chip. By executing the logical circuit across different physical qubit mappings 
  (layouts), it effectively scales the noise without adding extra gates or depth. \cite{b_sayapin2025}
\end{enumerate}


\paragraph{Extrapolation to the Zero-Noise Limit}
Once expectation values $C_\lambda(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ are collected at multiple noise factors
(typically $\lambda = 1, 3, 5$), a classical fitting model is applied. Common
fitting functions include linear, polynomial, exponential, or Richardson
extrapolation. The mitigated expectation value is determined by
taking the limit as the noise scale factor approaches zero: $C_{\text{ZNE}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) =
\lim_{\lambda \to 0} C_\lambda(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ \cite{b_chu2026, b_njiki2026, b_russo, b_liu2026, b_mohammadipour2025}.

\subsection{Synthesis and Research Gap}

The reviewed studies show that Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP) can represent linguistic
structure through quantum circuits and compositional models \cite{b_phukan2025, b_coecke2020, b_lorenz2023}.
Related works also show that noise affects variational quantum models in two ways: it can sometimes
support generalization, but it can also reduce trainability when the noise becomes too strong
\cite{b_zhu2026, b_xhelollari, b_cincio2021}. Meanwhile, Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) is commonly
used as an error mitigation method for estimating circuit outputs closer to the zero-noise case
\cite{b_wang2021, b_russo, b_sayapin2025, b_mohammadipour2025}.

Based on these studies, this paper focuses on a smaller and more controlled setting: a two-qubit
Variational Quantum Classifier for binary intent classification. The gap addressed in this study is
the limited evaluation of how depolarizing noise, measurement shot counts, and ZNE affect the accuracy
and variance of a simple VQC model under repeated Monte Carlo trials.

\section{Methodology}

This study uses a controlled simulation-based methodology to evaluate how
depolarizing noise, measurement shot count, and Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)
affect the accuracy and variance of a two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier
(VQC). The workflow follows a fixed-model evaluation design. The VQC is first
trained under a clean statevector setting. After training, the learned ansatz
parameters are extracted and reused during noisy and ZNE-based simulations. The
VQC is not retrained during noisy evaluation. This isolates the effects of
simulator noise, measurement shots, and ZNE from variation caused by repeated
model training.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation and Feature Extraction}

The experiment uses the Tanaos synthetic intent classifier dataset
\cite{b_tanaos_dataset}, a Hugging Face-hosted English text classification
dataset with numeric intent labels from 0 to 11. This study restricts the task
to two intent categories: Greeting and Farewell, corresponding to labels 0 and 1. The
selected subset is balanced by sampling 500 utterances from each class, producing
1{,}000 total samples. The balanced dataset is divided into training and testing
sets using an 80/20 stratified split, producing 800 training samples and 200
testing samples.

Text features are extracted using Term Frequency--Inverse Document Frequency
(TF-IDF) with English stopword removal and a maximum vocabulary size of 500.
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is then applied to reduce the TF-IDF feature
space to two components, matching the two-qubit input dimension of the VQC. The
TF-IDF vectorizer and PCA transformation are fitted only on the training set and
then applied to the test set to avoid information leakage.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Dataset Preparation and Feature Extraction}
\label{alg:data_features}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Dataset $D$, target labels $L=\{0,1\}$, samples per label $n=500$, test ratio $r=0.20$, base seed $s_0=42$
\Ensure Training features $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, test features $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, labels $y_{\mathrm{train}}$, $y_{\mathrm{test}}$
\State Load the Tanaos synthetic intent classifier dataset.
\State Standardize the label column name to \texttt{label}, if needed.
\State Filter $D$ to retain only \textit{Greeting} and \textit{Farewell}.
\For{each label $\ell \in L$}
    \State Randomly sample $n$ examples from class $\ell$ using seed $s_0$.
\EndFor
\State Concatenate the sampled class subsets into a balanced dataset $D_b$.
\State Split $D_b$ into train and test sets using stratified sampling with test ratio $r$ and seed $s_0$.
\State Fit a TF-IDF vectorizer on the training text with English stopword removal and a 500-feature limit.
\State Transform the training and test text into TF-IDF matrices.
\State Fit PCA with two components on the training TF-IDF matrix.
\State Transform both training and test TF-IDF matrices using the fitted PCA model.
\State \Return $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, $y_{\mathrm{train}}$, $y_{\mathrm{test}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Variational Quantum Classifier Training}

The VQC uses a \texttt{ZZFeatureMap} with one repetition for data encoding and a
\texttt{RealAmplitudes} ansatz with two repetitions for trainable quantum
parameters. This produces six trainable ansatz parameters. The model is trained
using the COBYLA optimizer with a maximum of 100 iterations. Clean training uses
Qiskit's \texttt{StatevectorSampler}; therefore, the training stage does not
include depolarizing noise or finite-shot sampling.

The classifier uses a binary parity interpretation. Even-parity measured
bitstrings are mapped to class 0, while odd-parity bitstrings are mapped to class
1. The same parity rule is also used during noisy simulation so that clean and
noisy evaluation follow the same label-mapping rule.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Clean VQC Training}
\label{alg:vqc_training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Training features $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, training labels $y_{\mathrm{train}}$
\Ensure Trained VQC model $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$
\State Construct a two-qubit \texttt{ZZFeatureMap} $U_{\phi}$ with one repetition.
\State Construct a two-qubit \texttt{RealAmplitudes} ansatz $U_{\theta}$ with two repetitions.
\State Define the parity interpretation $f(b)$.
\State Map even-parity bitstrings to class 0.
\State Map odd-parity bitstrings to class 1.
\State Initialize a VQC using $U_{\phi}$, $U_{\theta}$, COBYLA, \texttt{StatevectorSampler}, and parity output interpretation.
\State Train the VQC on $(X_{\mathrm{train}}, y_{\mathrm{train}})$.
\State \Return trained model $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Clean Baseline Evaluation}

After training, the clean VQC is evaluated on the held-out test set. This
baseline evaluation does not use simulated depolarizing noise or ZNE. Accuracy,
a classification report, and a confusion matrix are generated. The clean
baseline is used as a reference for checking whether the fixed noisy evaluator
remains consistent with the trained classifier when the base noise level is zero.

\subsection{Fixed-Model Noisy Evaluation}

Noisy evaluation is performed using Qiskit Aer with an optional depolarizing
noise model. For a noise level of 0.00, the simulator is run without a noise
model. For nonzero noise levels, single-qubit depolarizing errors are attached
to common one-qubit gates, and two-qubit depolarizing errors are attached to
common two-qubit gates.

The noisy evaluator uses the trained VQC as a fixed model. The trained ansatz
weights are extracted from the fitted VQC and reused for all noisy simulations.
The feature map and ansatz are composed into a single parameterized circuit,
measurement operations are added, and the circuit is decomposed before being
passed through a preset transpilation pass manager. The pass manager and Aer
backend are both seeded using the Monte Carlo trial seed. After transpilation,
each test sample is bound to the feature-map parameters, while the same trained
ansatz weights are bound to the ansatz parameters for every sample. The bound
test circuits are executed as a batch using the selected shot count. Measurement
counts are converted into class predictions using the same parity rule used by
the clean VQC.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Fixed-Model Noisy VQC Evaluation}
\label{alg:noisy_eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained VQC $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$, test features $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, test labels $y_{\mathrm{test}}$, noise level $\lambda$, shots $q$, seed $s$
\Ensure Noisy accuracy $A(\lambda,q,s)$

\State Extract trained ansatz weights $\hat{\theta}$ from $M$.
\State Compose the parameterized circuit $C$ by applying $U_{\phi}$ followed by $U_{\theta}$.
\State Add measurement operations to all qubits in $C$.
\State Decompose $C$ into simulator-compatible basis operations.

\If{$\lambda \leq 0$}
    \State Set the noise model $N \gets \texttt{None}$.
\Else
    \State Build a depolarizing noise model $N$ with error rate $\lambda$.
    \State Attach one-qubit depolarizing errors to common one-qubit gates.
    \State Attach two-qubit depolarizing errors to common two-qubit gates.
\EndIf

\State Initialize an Aer simulator backend using noise model $N$ and simulator seed $s$.
\State Generate a preset pass manager with optimization level 1, backend information, and transpiler seed $s$.
\State Transpile the decomposed parameterized circuit $C$ using the pass manager, producing $C'$.

\State Initialize an empty list of bound circuits $B$.
\For{each test sample $x_i \in X_{\mathrm{test}}$}
    \State Create a parameter assignment dictionary.
    \State Bind the components of $x_i$ to the feature-map parameters of $U_{\phi}$.
    \State Bind the trained weights $\hat{\theta}$ to the ansatz parameters of $U_{\theta}$.
    \State Assign all parameters to the transpiled circuit $C'$ to produce a bound circuit $C_i$.
    \State Append $C_i$ to $B$.
\EndFor

\State Execute all bound circuits $B$ as one batch on the Aer backend using $q$ shots.
\State Initialize an empty prediction list $\hat{Y}$.

\For{each circuit result index $i$}
    \State Retrieve the measured bitstring counts for bound circuit $C_i$.
    \State Remove spaces from each measured bitstring, if present.
    \State Compute the parity of each bitstring.
    \State Accumulate even-parity counts as class 0 and odd-parity counts as class 1.
    \If{class 0 count is greater than or equal to class 1 count}
        \State Predict class 0.
    \Else
        \State Predict class 1.
    \EndIf
    \State Append the predicted class to $\hat{Y}$.
\EndFor

\State Compute $A(\lambda,q,s)$ as the accuracy between $y_{\mathrm{test}}$ and $\hat{Y}$.
\State \Return $A(\lambda,q,s)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Noise and Shot-Count Sweep}

The experiment evaluates six discrete base depolarizing noise levels:

\[
0\%,\ 1\%,\ 3\%,\ 5\%,\ 7\%,\ 10\%.
\]

For each base noise level, the fixed VQC is evaluated under three measurement
shot counts:

\[
100,\ 1{,}000,\ 10{,}000.
\]

This produces 18 noise-shot configurations. Since the VQC is not retrained
across these configurations, changes in performance are attributed to simulated
noise level, measurement shot count, and Monte Carlo sampling seed.

\subsection{Zero-Noise Extrapolation}

Zero-Noise Extrapolation is applied as a post-processing error mitigation
procedure. For each base noise level $\lambda$, the trained classifier is
evaluated at three noise scale factors:

\[
1,\ 3,\ 5.
\]

The scaled noise levels are therefore

\[
\lambda,\ 3\lambda,\ 5\lambda.
\]

The accuracy measured at scale factor 1 is treated as the raw noisy accuracy.
The ZNE estimate is obtained by fitting a linear model between scaled noise
levels and observed accuracies. The fitted intercept at zero noise is used as
the ZNE-mitigated accuracy estimate. If all scaled noise levels are identical,
as in the 0.00 base-noise case, the measured accuracy is returned directly. The
extrapolated accuracy is clipped to the interval $[0,1]$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Zero-Noise Extrapolation for One Configuration}
\label{alg:zne}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained VQC $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$, test set $(X_{\mathrm{test}}, y_{\mathrm{test}})$, base noise $\lambda$, shots $q$, seed $s$, scale factors $S=\{1,3,5\}$
\Ensure Raw accuracy $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, ZNE accuracy $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, ZNE delta $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$
\State Initialize empty lists $N \gets [\ ]$ and $A \gets [\ ]$.
\For{each scale factor $k \in S$}
    \State Compute scaled noise $\lambda_k \gets \min(k\lambda, 1.0)$.
    \State Evaluate the fixed VQC under $\lambda_k$ using Algorithm~\ref{alg:noisy_eval}.
    \State Append $\lambda_k$ to $N$ and the measured accuracy $A_k$ to $A$.
\EndFor
\State Set $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$ to the accuracy measured at scale factor 1.
\If{all values in $N$ are identical}
    \State Set $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets A_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
\Else
    \State Fit a linear model $A_k = m\lambda_k + b$ over the pairs $(N,A)$.
    \State Set $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets b$.
    \State Clip $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ to the interval $[0,1]$.
\EndIf
\State Compute $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - A_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
\State \Return $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Monte Carlo Evaluation Design}

A base random seed of 42 is used for reproducibility. This seed controls dataset
sampling, the train-test split, and the initial Python and NumPy random states.
For the Monte Carlo sweep, the simulator seed is not kept constant. For each
trial, the seed is computed as

\[
s_t = s_0 + t,
\]

where $s_0=42$ and $t$ is the trial index. Thus, each noise-shot configuration
is evaluated across 1{,}000 deterministic but distinct simulator seeds. The same
seed sequence is reused across different noise-shot configurations, keeping
comparisons reproducible and paired by trial index.

Because there are six base noise levels, three shot counts, and 1{,}000 trials
per configuration, the complete sweep contains

\[
6 \times 3 \times 1{,}000 = 18{,}000
\]

ZNE evaluation jobs. Each ZNE job evaluates three scaled noise levels, so the
full sweep performs

\[
18{,}000 \times 3 = 54{,}000
\]

scaled noisy evaluations.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo ZNE Sweep}
\label{alg:mc_sweep}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Noise levels $\Lambda=\{0.00,0.01,0.03,0.05,0.07,0.10\}$, shot counts $Q=\{100,1000,10000\}$, trials $T=1000$, base seed $s_0=42$, scale factors $S=\{1,3,5\}$
\Ensure Raw trial table $R$
\State Initialize empty result table $R$.
\For{each base noise level $\lambda \in \Lambda$}
    \For{each shot count $q \in Q$}
        \For{$t = 0$ to $T-1$}
            \State Set trial seed $s_t \gets s_0 + t$.
            \State Run Algorithm~\ref{alg:zne} using $\lambda$, $q$, $s_t$, and $S$.
            \State Store $\lambda$, $q$, $s_t$, scaled noise levels, scaled accuracies, $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, and $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ in $R$.
        \EndFor
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State Sort $R$ by base noise level, shot count, and seed.
\State \Return $R$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo Result Summarization and Output Generation}
\label{alg:mc_postprocessing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Raw trial table $R$
\Ensure Grouped summary table $G$, saved CSV files, and result figures
\State Group $R$ by base noise level and shot count.
\State Compute mean raw accuracy for each group.
\State Compute mean ZNE accuracy for each group.
\State Compute raw and ZNE accuracy variance for each group.
\State Compute raw and ZNE accuracy standard deviation for each group.
\State Compute mean ZNE delta for each group.
\State Compute minimum and maximum ZNE accuracy for each group.
\State Count the number of Monte Carlo trials in each group.
\State Store the grouped statistics in summary table $G$.
\State Generate the raw-versus-ZNE mean accuracy plot.
\State Generate the raw-versus-ZNE accuracy variance plot.
\State Generate the ZNE accuracy gain plot.
\State Save $R$ and $G$ as timestamped CSV files.
\State \Return $G$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The Monte Carlo sweep produces the raw trial-level ZNE results. These raw results
are then converted into a dataframe, grouped by base noise level and shot count,
summarized statistically, plotted, and saved as timestamped CSV files in separate
post-processing steps.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The primary metric is classification accuracy on the held-out test set. For each
noise-shot configuration, the 1{,}000 Monte Carlo trials are summarized using
mean raw accuracy, mean ZNE accuracy, raw accuracy variance, ZNE accuracy
variance, raw standard deviation, ZNE standard deviation, mean ZNE delta, minimum
ZNE accuracy, and maximum ZNE accuracy.

The ZNE accuracy delta is defined as

\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}} = A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - A_{\mathrm{raw}},
\]

where $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ is the extrapolated ZNE accuracy and
$A_{\mathrm{raw}}$ is the accuracy measured at the unscaled base noise level.
The raw Monte Carlo outputs and grouped summary statistics are saved as
timestamped CSV files to prevent accidental overwriting of previous experiment
outputs.

18{,}000 ZNE evaluation runs.

in this fixed-model evaluation. However, this should be interpreted cautiously,
since the experiment uses a small two-qubit classifier, finite-shot majority
decoding, and a reduced two-dimensional feature representation.

\subsection{Effect of Shot Count on Stability}

Increasing the shot count reduced the Monte Carlo spread of the raw and ZNE
accuracy estimates. Averaged across all noise levels, the raw standard deviation
was approximately 2.85 percentage points at 100 shots, 2.11 percentage points at
1{,}000 shots, and 1.02 percentage points at 10{,}000 shots. The same pattern
appeared for ZNE, although ZNE remained more variable than the corresponding raw
estimate.

For every nonzero-noise configuration, the ZNE variance was higher than the raw
accuracy variance. This is expected from the design of the experiment because the
ZNE estimate is computed from multiple noisy evaluations at scaled noise levels.
The extrapolated intercept can improve the mean estimate, but it can also
amplify trial-level variation.

\subsection{ZNE Accuracy Gain}

Figure~\ref{fig:zne_accuracy_gain} shows the mean ZNE accuracy gain for each
noise-shot configuration. The gain was small at 0.01 noise, became more visible
at moderate noise levels, and remained positive at 0.10 noise. The strongest
gain occurred at 0.07 noise with 10{,}000 shots.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includesvg[width=\columnwidth]{../notebooks/results/figures/zne_accuracy_gain_20260526_192753_220013}

These results indicate that ZNE improved the mean accuracy of the fixed trained
VQC under simulated depolarizing noise, but the improvement came with increased
variance. Therefore, the results support a limited conclusion: in this controlled
two-qubit simulation, linear ZNE can improve average noisy evaluation accuracy,
but it does not uniformly stabilize the classifier output. The method is better
understood as an accuracy-bias correction technique rather than a general
variance-reduction technique.
