% -------------------------------------------------------
% Abstract
% -------------------------------------------------------
\def\myabstract{%
\mdseries
This study evaluates a simple \textbf{2-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)}
for binary intent classification under simulated Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum
(NISQ) conditions. The experiment uses a focused subset of the
\textbf{Tanaos synthetic intent classifier v1} dataset \cite{b_tanaos_dataset}.
The classes are limited to the \emph{Greeting} and \emph{Farewell} intent groups.
The subset is balanced by sampling 500 text patterns from each class, producing
1{,}000 total samples for research purposes. Text utterances are represented using
Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) 
and reduced to two principal components through Principal Component
Analysis (PCA), matching the two-qubit input size of the classifier.

The VQC has a ZZFeatureMap for feature encoding that uses a single repetition, and a
RealAmplitudes ansatz with two repetitions. This resulted with a quantum circuit having six trainable
parameters. After training, the model would be then evaluated under simulated
depolarizing noise. For this particular research, \textbf{Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)} 
is utilized as the primary error-mitigation method. 

The evaluation uses depolarizing noise
levels of 0\%, 1\%, 3\%, 5\%, 7\%, and 10\%, with shot counts of
100, 1{,}000, and 10{,}000. In each noise-shot pair configuration, a thousand (1{,}000)
seed-varied from a specified seed-base (the seed base is 42 for this specific research) of Monte Carlo 
trials would be performed to estimate the mean accuracy and
the variance caused by finite-shot sampling and simulated depolarizing noise.

In the recorded experiment, the clean statevector VQC achieved
60.00\% accuracy on the test set. Across nonzero-noise configurations, the zero-noise extrapolated accuracy
estimate improved over the raw noisy estimate by an average of
approximately 1.45\%, with the largest gain of 3.67\%
at 0.07 depolarizing noise and 10,000 shots. However, the extrapolated
estimates also showed higher variance than the corresponding raw
estimates, indicating that the method improved the mean estimate but did
not stabilize the outputs because it had more variability.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Recent studies have explored the use of variational quantum models for
classification tasks, including applications related to Natural Language
Processing (NLP). However, current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ)
devices still remains affected by decoherence, gate imperfections, crosstalk, and
readout or measurement noise, which can make quantum model outputs unstable for
any practical usage \cite{b_njiki2026, b_koenig2024}.

Within NLP, Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP) has been studied as a
way of connecting language representation with quantum models. This has been done 
through compositional sentence representations and variational
circuits \cite{b_phukan2025, b_guarasci2022, b_lorenz2023}. The present study
does not implement a full compositional QNLP pipeline. Instead, it uses a
smaller VQC-based text classification pipeline so that the effect of noise, shot
count, and ZNE can be isolated under controlled simulation.

Towards small variational quantum classifiers, noise is especially important because
the measured output depends on repeated executions. Finite measurement
shots can introduce sampling variation, and depolarizing noise can alter the
observed bitstring distribution. These effects make it difficult to interpret a
single noisy evaluation as a stable estimate of classifier performance. Because
of this, repeated simulations which is to be done via Monte Carlo framework are useful for measuring how much the
classifier output varies under the experimental configuration.

Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) is commonly
used to estimate a circuit output closer to zero-noise case. This is done by evaluating
the same circuit under increased noise levels and extrapolating the measured
values back toward the zero-noise limit \cite{b_koenig2024, b_mohammadipour2025}. 
For this study, ZNE is used as the primary
post-processing method for noisy evaluation. The VQC is trained once under a
clean setting, and the trained parameters are reused during noisy and
ZNE-based simulations.

This study uses a Monte Carlo framework to examine how simulated
depolarizing noise, measurement shot counts, and Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)
affect the behavior of a fixed two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC).
The said VQC will specialize for binary intent classification. The pipeline evaluates 1{,}000 seed-varied
Monte Carlo trials for each configuration of depolarizing noise at 0\%, 1\%,
3\%, 5\%, 7\%, and 10\%, then paired with measurement shot counts of 100,
1{,}000, and 10{,}000. This repeated evaluation is used to estimate the mean
accuracy and the variance introduced by finite-shot sampling and simulated
depolarizing noise.

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:related_literature}

\subsection{Quantum NLP and Compositional Background}

A major foundation for QNLP is the DisCoCat framework, which
combines distributional word meanings with grammatical composition using
category-theoretic structure \cite{b_coeckesadrzadeh2010}. Later works argued
that this compositional structure has a natural connection to quantum
computation and can be mapped into quantum algorithms or near-term quantum
circuits \cite{b_zengcoecke2016, b_coecke2020}.Practical QNLP experiments
have demonstrated small sentence classification tasks on NISQ hardware, while
survey work highlights both the promise and the current limitations of quantum
models for language specific tasks \cite{b_lorenz2023, b_guarasci2022, b_phukan2025}.

This study is related to QNLP mainly in terms of task motivation. It does not
use grammatical reductions, DisCoCat diagrams, or direct sentence-to-circuit
conversion. Instead, text datas are processed using classical feature
extraction, reduced to two PCA components, and then passed into a small VQC for
classification. This simpler setup was chosen so the experiment could focus more
directly on noise sensitivity, finite-shot variation, and the effect of ZNE.

\subsection{Small Variational Quantum Classifiers}

Variational Quantum Classifiers use parameterized quantum circuits for supervised
learning. They are usually built from three parts: a feature map that encodes
classical data, an ansatz with trainable parameters, and a measurement rule that
maps circuit outputs to class labels \cite{b_seilkhan2026, b_higgs}. Since
quantum models are limited by qubit count and circuit depth, dimensionality
reduction is commonly needed when classical input data has more features than
the available number of qubits \cite{b_higgs}.

Related VQC studies show that PCA and other reduction methods are often used to map classical
features into a smaller latent space before quantum encoding \cite{b_higgs}. Other benchmark
studies on low-dimensional classification tasks show that two-qubit or shallow VQC models can
be useful for controlled experiments, even when they do not yet show a clear advantage over
classical models \cite{b_seilkhan2026}.
These points support the design choice of using a two-dimensional PCA representation and a
two-qubit VQC in this study.

The choice of ansatz is also important in QNLP-style and VQC-style models.
Different circuit mappings affect performance, trainability, and
susceptibility to barren plateau behavior \cite{b_khatri2022}.

\subsection{Noise, Trainability, and Measurement Variation}

Noise still remains a central limitation of quantum computers. In variational quantum
algorithms, hardware noise and finite measurement shots can affect the measured output of the
circuit. It can make a single evaluation unstable. Noise can also interact with trainability.
Recent results from published papers suggest that effects of noise is not uniformly harmful: in some quantum machine
learning settings, moderate noise can behave like an implicit regularizer. This is observed in under-optimized
models, while better-converged models may degrade performance under the same noise conditions
\cite{b_zhu2026}. At the same time, other work shows that noise can flatten variational cost
landscapes and reduce trainability, especially in noise-induced barren plateau regimes
\cite{b_wang2021}.

Related work on variational quantum classifiers has also explored noise-based
optimization as a way to improve training behavior under barren plateau concerns
\cite{b_lung2026}.

Studies on hybrid quantum neural networks show that the
effects of error mitigation depends on the noise model, the circuit, and the training or evaluation
setting \cite{b_njiki2026}. These works motivate the need to evaluate not only the mean accuracy
of a noisy VQC, but also the variance of its output under repeated simulation trials 
which is what the Monte Carlo framework would be used for.

\subsection{Zero-Noise Extrapolation}

Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) is an error mitigation method. It estimates a zero-noise output
by evaluating a circuit at an increased noise levels and then extrapolating the resulting measurements
back toward the zero-noise limit. Several ZNE approaches have been proposed, including methods
based on circuit folding, inverted circuits, layout permutations, and polynomial or regression-based
extrapolation \cite{b_koenig2024, b_sayapin2025, b_mohammadipour2025}.

This paper uses a simpler ZNE setup. It does not compare several ZNE
methods. Instead, it increases the simulated depolarizing noise level using scale
factors of 1, 3, and 5. A linear fit is then used to estimate the accuracy at the
zero-noise limit. This setup keeps the experiment focused on one question: How
ZNE affects the measured accuracy and variance of a fixed two-qubit VQC across
repeated Monte Carlo trials?

\subsection{Synthesis and Research Gap}

Building the grounds upon this, the gap the paper will address is narrow and implementation-specific: There is
limited evaluation of how simulated depolarizing noise, measurement shot count, and linear ZNE levels
affect the accuracy and variance of a fixed two-qubit VQC for binary intent classification. This
study shall address that gap through Monte Carlo simulation design and seed-varied
evaluations across noise-shot pair configurations.

\section{Methodology}

This methodology follows the implementation used in the Jupyter Notebook which will be in a form of a 
public Github repository \cite{b_project_repo}. The classifier
is trained once using a clean statevector setting.
It will be then reused for all the noisy and
ZNE evaluations. After training, the learned ansatz parameters are kept fixed.
No retraining shall be performed during the Monte Carlo processes. With this setup,
changes in measured accuracy come from the simulator seed, shot count,
depolarizing noise level, and the ZNE steps rather than from repeated
optimizer runs.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation and Feature Extraction}

The experiment in the Jupyter Notebook uses the Tanaos synthetic intent classifier dataset
\cite{b_tanaos_dataset}, a Hugging Face-hosted text classification
dataset with numeric intent labels from 0 to 11. The primary language of this dataset is English. 
This study restricts the task to two intent categories: Greeting and Farewell, their corresponding of labels 0 and 1. 
The selected subset is balanced by sampling 500 patterns from each class. Together, the two classes produced
1{,}000 total samples. The balanced dataset is divided into training and testing
sets using an 80/20 stratified split. This would then produce 800 training samples and 200
testing samples.

Text features are extracted using Term Frequency--Inverse Document Frequency
(TF-IDF) with English stopword removal. It will have a maximum vocabulary size of 500.
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) would be then applied to reduce the TF-IDF feature
space to two components. This matches the two-qubit input dimension of the VQC but it 
also limits how much information from the original patterns can be
used by the classifier. The TF-IDF vectorizer and PCA transformation are fitted
only on the training set and then applied to the test set to avoid information
leakage and keep the evaluation based on unseen test data.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Dataset Preparation and Feature Extraction}
\label{alg:data_features}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Dataset $D$, target labels $L=\{0,1\}$, samples per label $n=500$, test ratio $r=0.20$, base seed $s_0=42$
\Ensure Training features $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, test features $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, labels $y_{\mathrm{train}}$, $y_{\mathrm{test}}$
\State Load the Tanaos synthetic intent classifier dataset.
\State Standardize the label column name to \texttt{label}, if needed.
\State Filter $D$ to retain only \textit{Greeting} and \textit{Farewell}.
\For{each label $\ell \in L$}
    \State Randomly sample $n$ examples from class $\ell$ using seed $s_0$.
\EndFor
\State Concatenate the sampled class subsets into a balanced dataset $D_b$.
\State Split $D_b$ into train and test sets using stratified sampling with test ratio $r$ and seed $s_0$.
\State Fit a TF-IDF vectorizer on the training text with English stopword removal and a 500-feature limit.
\State Transform the training and test text into TF-IDF matrices.
\State Fit PCA with two components on the training TF-IDF matrix.
\State Transform both training and test TF-IDF matrices using the fitted PCA model.
\State \Return $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, $y_{\mathrm{train}}$, $y_{\mathrm{test}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Variational Quantum Classifier Training}

The VQC uses a \texttt{ZZFeatureMap} with one repetition for data encoding and a
\texttt{RealAmplitudes} ansatz with two repetitions for trainable quantum
parameters. This produces six trainable ansatz parameters. The model is trained
using the COBYLA optimizer with a maximum of 100 iterations. Clean training uses Qiskit's 
\texttt{StatevectorSampler}; therefore, the training stage does not
use the Aer depolarizing-noise model or the noisy-evaluation shot-count sweep.

The classifier uses a binary parity interpretation. Even-parity measured
bitstrings are mapped to class 0, while odd-parity bitstrings are mapped to class
1. The same parity rule is also used during noisy simulation so that clean and
noisy evaluation follow the same label-mapping rule.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Clean VQC Training}
\label{alg:vqc_training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Training features $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, training labels $y_{\mathrm{train}}$
\Ensure Trained VQC model $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$
\State Construct a two-qubit \texttt{ZZFeatureMap} $U_{\phi}$ with one repetition.
\State Construct a two-qubit \texttt{RealAmplitudes} ansatz $U_{\theta}$ with two repetitions.
\State Define the parity interpretation $f(b)$.
\State Map even-parity bitstrings to class 0.
\State Map odd-parity bitstrings to class 1.
\State Initialize a VQC using $U_{\phi}$, $U_{\theta}$, COBYLA, \texttt{StatevectorSampler}, and parity output interpretation.
\State Train the VQC on $(X_{\mathrm{train}}, y_{\mathrm{train}})$.
\State \Return trained model $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Clean Baseline Evaluation}

After training, the clean VQC is evaluated on the held-out test set. This
baseline evaluation does not use simulated depolarizing noise or ZNE. Accuracy,
a classification report, and a confusion matrix are generated. The clean
baseline is used as a reference for checking whether the fixed noisy evaluator
remains consistent with the trained classifier when the base noise level is zero.

\subsection{Fixed-Model Noisy Evaluation}

Noisy evaluation is performed using Qiskit Aer with an optional depolarizing
noise model. For a noise level of 0.00, the simulator is run without a noise
model. For nonzero noise levels, single-qubit depolarizing errors are attached
to common one-qubit gates, and two-qubit depolarizing errors are attached to
common two-qubit gates.

The noisy evaluator uses the trained VQC as a fixed model. The trained ansatz
weights are extracted from the fitted VQC and reused for all noisy simulations.
The feature map and ansatz are composed into a single parameterized circuit,
measurement operations are added, and the circuit is decomposed before being
passed through a preset transpilation pass manager. The pass manager and Aer
backend are both seeded using the Monte Carlo trial seed. After transpilation,
each test sample is bound to the feature-map parameters, while the same trained
ansatz weights are bound to the ansatz parameters for every sample. The bound
test circuits are executed as a batch using the selected shot count. Measurement
counts are converted into class predictions using the same parity rule used by
the clean VQC.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Fixed-Model Noisy VQC Evaluation}
\label{alg:noisy_eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained VQC $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$, test features $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, test labels $y_{\mathrm{test}}$, noise level $\lambda$, shots $q$, seed $s$
\Ensure Noisy accuracy $A(\lambda,q,s)$

\State Extract trained ansatz weights $\hat{\theta}$ from $M$.
\State Compose the parameterized circuit $C$ by applying $U_{\phi}$ followed by $U_{\theta}$.
\State Add measurement operations to all qubits in $C$.
\State Decompose $C$ into simulator-compatible basis operations.

\If{$\lambda \leq 0$}
    \State Set the noise model $N \gets \texttt{None}$.
\Else
    \State Build a depolarizing noise model $N$ with error rate $\lambda$.
    \State Attach one-qubit depolarizing errors to common one-qubit gates.
    \State Attach two-qubit depolarizing errors to common two-qubit gates.
\EndIf

\State Initialize an Aer simulator backend using noise model $N$ and simulator seed $s$.
\State Generate a preset pass manager with optimization level 1, backend information, and transpiler seed $s$.
\State Transpile the decomposed parameterized circuit $C$ using the pass manager, producing $C'$.

\State Initialize an empty list of bound circuits $B$.
\For{each test sample $x_i \in X_{\mathrm{test}}$}
    \State Create a parameter assignment dictionary.
    \State Bind the components of $x_i$ to the feature-map parameters of $U_{\phi}$.
    \State Bind the trained weights $\hat{\theta}$ to the ansatz parameters of $U_{\theta}$.
    \State Assign all parameters to the transpiled circuit $C'$ to produce a bound circuit $C_i$.
    \State Append $C_i$ to $B$.
\EndFor

\State Execute all bound circuits $B$ as one batch on the Aer backend using $q$ shots.
\State Initialize an empty prediction list $\hat{Y}$.

\For{each circuit result index $i$}
    \State Retrieve the measured bitstring counts for bound circuit $C_i$.
    \State Remove spaces from each measured bitstring, if present.
    \State Compute the parity of each bitstring.
    \State Accumulate even-parity counts as class 0 and odd-parity counts as class 1.
    \If{class 0 count is greater than or equal to class 1 count}
        \State Predict class 0.
    \Else
        \State Predict class 1.
    \EndIf
    \State Append the predicted class to $\hat{Y}$.
\EndFor

\State Compute $A(\lambda,q,s)$ as the accuracy between $y_{\mathrm{test}}$ and $\hat{Y}$.
\State \Return $A(\lambda,q,s)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Noise and Shot-Count Sweep}

The experiment evaluates six discrete base depolarizing noise levels:

\[
0\%,\ 1\%,\ 3\%,\ 5\%,\ 7\%,\ 10\%.
\]

For each base noise level, the fixed VQC is evaluated under three measurement
shot counts:

\[
100,\ 1{,}000,\ 10{,}000.
\]

This produces 18 noise-shot configurations. Since the VQC is not retrained
across these configurations, changes in performance are attributed to simulated
noise level, measurement shot count, and Monte Carlo sampling seed.

\subsection{Zero-Noise Extrapolation}

Zero-Noise Extrapolation is applied as a post-processing error mitigation
procedure. In general, ZNE estimates a zero-noise value by evaluating a circuit
at multiple amplified noise levels and fitting or extrapolating the measured
outputs back to the zero-noise limit \cite{b_koenig2024, b_mohammadipour2025}.
For each base noise level $\lambda$, the trained classifier is evaluated at
three noise scale factors:

\[
1,\ 3,\ 5.
\]

The scaled noise levels are therefore

\[
\lambda,\ 3\lambda,\ 5\lambda.
\]

The accuracy measured at scale factor 1 is treated as the raw noisy accuracy.
The ZNE estimate is obtained by fitting a linear model between scaled noise
levels and observed accuracies. The fitted intercept at zero noise is used as
the ZNE-mitigated accuracy estimate. If all scaled noise levels are identical,
as in the 0.00 base-noise case, the measured accuracy is returned directly. The
extrapolated accuracy is clipped to the interval $[0,1]$.

In this implementation, extrapolation is applied to the aggregate
classification accuracy rather than to each circuit expectation value or
sample-level class probability. Therefore, the procedure is best understood
as an accuracy-level ZNE-style estimate. This design is useful for studying
how the measured classifier accuracy changes under controlled noise
scaling, but it should not be interpreted as a full observable-level ZNE
prediction pipeline.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Zero-Noise Extrapolation for One Configuration}
\label{alg:zne}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained VQC $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$, test set $(X_{\mathrm{test}}, y_{\mathrm{test}})$, base noise $\lambda$, shots $q$, seed $s$, scale factors $S=\{1,3,5\}$
\Ensure Raw accuracy $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, ZNE accuracy $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, ZNE delta $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$
\State Initialize empty lists $N \gets [\ ]$ and $A \gets [\ ]$.
\For{each scale factor $k \in S$}
    \State Compute scaled noise $\lambda_k \gets \min(k\lambda, 1.0)$.
    \State Evaluate the fixed VQC under $\lambda_k$ using Algorithm~\ref{alg:noisy_eval}.
    \State Append $\lambda_k$ to $N$ and the measured accuracy $A_k$ to $A$.
\EndFor
\State Set $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$ to the accuracy measured at scale factor 1.
\If{all values in $N$ are identical}
    \State Set $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets A_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
\Else
    \State Fit a linear model $A_k = m\lambda_k + b$ over the pairs $(N,A)$.
    \State Set $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets b$.
    \State Clip $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ to the interval $[0,1]$.
\EndIf
\State Compute $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - A_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
\State \Return $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Monte Carlo Evaluation Design}

A base random seed of 42 is used for reproducibility in the Jupyter Notebook. This seed controls dataset
sampling, the train-test split, and the initial Python and NumPy random states.
For the Monte Carlo processes, the simulator seed would not be kept constant. 
Each trial computes the seed as:

\[
s_t = s_0 + t,
\]

where $s_0=42$ and $t$ is the trial index. This makes each noise-shot pair configuration
is being evaluated across 1{,}000 deterministic but distinct simulator seeds. 
Knowing that this research would have six (6) base noise levels, three (3) shot counts, and 1{,}000 trials
per configuration, the complete sweep will become:

\[
6 \times 3 \times 1{,}000 = 18{,}000
\]

ZNE evaluation jobs. And since ZNE jobs evaluate on three scaled noise levels (these are 
multiplicative noise levels 1, 3, and 5), the full process performs

\[
18{,}000 \times 3 = 54{,}000
\]

scaled noisy evaluations.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo ZNE Sweep}
\label{alg:mc_sweep}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Noise levels $\Lambda=\{0.00,0.01,0.03,0.05,0.07,0.10\}$, shot counts $Q=\{100,1000,10000\}$, trials $T=1000$, base seed $s_0=42$, scale factors $S=\{1,3,5\}$
\Ensure Raw trial table $R$
\State Initialize empty result table $R$.
\For{each base noise level $\lambda \in \Lambda$}
    \For{each shot count $q \in Q$}
        \For{$t = 0$ to $T-1$}
            \State Set trial seed $s_t \gets s_0 + t$.
            \State Run Algorithm~\ref{alg:zne} using $\lambda$, $q$, $s_t$, and $S$.
            \State Store $\lambda$, $q$, $s_t$, scaled noise levels, scaled accuracies, $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, and $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ in $R$.
        \EndFor
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State Sort $R$ by base noise level, shot count, and seed.
\State \Return $R$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo Result Summarization and Output Generation}
\label{alg:mc_postprocessing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Raw trial table $R$
\Ensure Grouped summary table $G$, saved CSV files, and result figures
\State Group $R$ by base noise level and shot count.
\State Compute mean raw accuracy for each group.
\State Compute mean ZNE accuracy for each group.
\State Compute raw and ZNE accuracy variance for each group.
\State Compute raw and ZNE accuracy standard deviation for each group.
\State Compute mean ZNE delta for each group.
\State Compute minimum and maximum ZNE accuracy for each group.
\State Count the number of Monte Carlo trials in each group.
\State Store the grouped statistics in summary table $G$.
\State Generate the raw-versus-ZNE mean accuracy plot.
\State Generate the raw-versus-ZNE accuracy variance plot.
\State Generate the ZNE accuracy gain plot.
\State Save $R$ and $G$ as timestamped CSV files.
\State \Return $G$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The Monte Carlo sweep produces the raw trial-level ZNE results. These raw results
are then converted into a dataframe, grouped by base noise level and shot count,
summarized statistically, plotted, and saved as timestamped CSV files in separate
post-processing steps.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The primary metric to compare is classification accuracy on the test set. It
will be exported to a timestamped csv files after the Monte Carlo Process.
The purpose of timestamped CSVs is to prevent accidental overwriting of previous
experiment. Each noise-shot configuration; the 1{,}000 Monte Carlo trials are summarized using
mean raw accuracy, mean ZNE accuracy, raw accuracy variance, ZNE accuracy
variance, raw standard deviation, ZNE standard deviation, mean ZNE delta, minimum
ZNE accuracy, and maximum ZNE accuracy.

The ZNE accuracy delta is basically defined as

\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}} = A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - A_{\mathrm{raw}},
\]

where $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ is the extrapolated ZNE accuracy and
$A_{\mathrm{raw}}$ is the accuracy measured at the unscaled base noise level.

\section{Results}

This section discusses the clean baseline result and the ZNE results from the
two-qubit VQC experiment. To recap, the model was tested using six depolarizing noise
levels and three shot counts. For every noise and shot-count pair, 1{,}000 Monte
Carlo trials were run. This gives 18{,}000 ZNE jobs in total. Since each ZNE job
uses three scaled noise levels, the whole experiment performs 54{,}000 scaled
noisy evaluations.

\subsection{Clean Baseline Performance}

In the recorded experiment run, the clean statevector VQC reached 60.00\%
accuracy on the 200-sample test set. This test set was not used during training,
so it served as the unseen data for checking the model. It was also balanced,
with 100 Greeting samples and 100 Farewell samples. From these, the classifier
correctly predicted 41 Greeting samples and 79 Farewell samples. This shows that
the model performed better on the Farewell class than on the Greeting class in
the clean baseline test.

The result may change slightly if the experiment would rerun. In testing upon repeated runs,
the clean accuracy usually stayed around 60\% to 62\%. This variation is expected
because the result can depend on the sampled train-test split, the optimizer
behavior, and the software execution environment. The 60.00\% value reported here
comes from the saved run used for the figures, tables, and CSV files in this
paper.

\subsection{Effect of Shot Count on Stability}

The shot count mostly affected how spread out the Monte Carlo results were. In this case,
the average standard deviation across the noise levels was about 2.85\% at 100 shots, 
2.11\% at 1{,}000 shots, and 1.02\% at 10{,}000 shots in raw accuracy. This
shows that using more shot counts made the results stable. The same is also true for 
ZNE results. However, in the nonzero-noise cases,
their standard deviations were higher than the raw accuracy standard
deviations.

evaluations. In this experiment, ZNE increased the mean accuracy estimate, but it
also made the trial-level results more spread out.

Figure~\ref{fig:zne_accuracy_gain} shows the mean ZNE accuracy gain for each
noise-shot configuration. The gain was smallest at 0.01 depolarizing noise and
became larger at moderate noise levels. The largest gain in the saved run
occurred at 0.07 noise with 10{,}000 shots, where mean raw accuracy was 62.72\%
and mean ZNE accuracy was 66.39\%. At 0.10 noise, the gain was still positive,
but it was lower than the peak observed at 0.07 noise.

ZNE procedure used here is better described as an accuracy-level correction to
mean estimate, not as a method for stabilizing individual noisy classifier
runs.

\subsection{Limitations}

Some parts of the implementation limit how much the results can be generalized.
The task only used two intent classes. Each text sample is reduced to two
PCA components before being encoded into the quantum circuit. This makes the data
fit the nature of two-qubit VQC, but it also means that the original text-classification
task becomes a much smaller, low-dimensional experiment \cite{b_higgs,
b_seilkhan2026}.

The noise model is also simplified. The noisy runs use simulated depolarizing
noise, not a device-specific hardware model. Real NISQ processors can include
readout error, relaxation, dephasing, crosstalk, and uneven gate fidelities.
Those effects may change both the classifier output and the behavior of ZNE
\cite{b_njiki2026, b_koenig2024}.

The ZNE step is also limited. In standard ZNE, the extrapolation is usually done
on measured circuit values, such as observable expectation values, before the
final task metric is computed \cite{b_koenig2024, b_mohammadipour2025}. For this
specific study, the extrapolation is done on the overall classification accuracy instead.
Because of this, results should be understood as an accuracy-level ZNE-style
estimate, not as a full observable-level ZNE pipeline.

Finally, this paper does not and will never claim quantum advantage over classical classifiers.
It only reports a controlled simulation of a small VQC under depolarizing noise,
finite-shot samplings, and linear extrapolation. Larger intent sets, classical
baselines using the same PCA features, other ansatz variants, and circuit-level
ZNE should be tested before making broader claims
\cite{b_lorenz2023, b_seilkhan2026, b_khatri2022}.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper tested a fixed two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier for binary
intent classification. It used simulated depolarizing noise and different
measurement shot counts. The classifier was trained once in a clean
statevector setting. After training, the same learned parameters were used for
all noisy evaluations and ZNE evaluations.

In the saved experiment run, the clean statevector classifier reached 60.00\%
accuracy on the test set which were the unseen data, not to be mixed up with the trained data. 
Across the fifteen nonzero-noise simulated configurations, 
ZNE estimates were higher than their corresponding raw
noisy estimates by an average of about 1.45\%. The largest gain
was 3.67\% at 0.07 simulated depolarizing noise with 10{,}000 shot counts.

The results also show a limitation of the method. In every nonzero-noise setting,
ZNE had a higher standard deviation than the raw accuracy estimate. This means
that although ZNE improved the mean accuracy estimate, it did not make the
individual trial results more stable. In this setup, ZNE is better understood as
a correction to the average estimate, not as a method for reducing variance.

The findings are limited by the subsets of two-class dataset, the two-qubit count circuit, the
simulated depolarizing noise model, and the use of accuracy-level extrapolation. 
Future work should test larger intent class sets, compare against
classical baselines using the same PCA features, evaluating with use of other quantum ansatz
variants, and apply ZNE to circuit-level quantities before computing final
classification accuracy.
