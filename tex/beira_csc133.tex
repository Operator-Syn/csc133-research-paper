% -------------------------------------------------------
% Abstract
% -------------------------------------------------------
\def\myabstract{%
\mdseries
This study evaluates a simple \textbf{2-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)}
for binary intent classification under simulated Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum
(NISQ) conditions. The experiment uses a focused subset of the
\textbf{Tanaos synthetic intent classifier v1} dataset \cite{b_tanaos_dataset},
limited to the \emph{Greeting} and \emph{Farewell} intent categories.
The subset is balanced by sampling 500 utterances from each class, producing
1{,}000 total samples for the experiment. Text utterances are represented using
TF-IDF and reduced to two principal components through Principal Component
Analysis (PCA), matching the two-qubit input size of the classifier.

The VQC uses a ZZFeatureMap with one repetition for feature encoding and a
RealAmplitudes ansatz with two repetitions, resulting in six trainable
parameters. After clean training, the trained model is evaluated under simulated
depolarizing noise. \textbf{Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)} is then applied as a
post-processing error mitigation method. The evaluation uses depolarizing noise
levels of 0\%, 1\%, 3\%, 5\%, 7\%, and 10\%, with measurement shot counts of
100, 1{,}000, and 10{,}000. For each noise-shot configuration, 1{,}000
seed-varied Monte Carlo trials are performed to estimate the mean accuracy and
variance caused by finite-shot sampling and simulated depolarizing noise.

In the recorded experiment run, the clean statevector VQC achieved
60.00\% accuracy on the held-out test set. Across nonzero-noise
configurations, the zero-noise extrapolated accuracy estimate improved
over the raw noisy estimate by an average of approximately 1.45
percentage points, with the largest gain of 3.67 percentage points at
0.07 depolarizing noise and 10{,}000 shots. However, the extrapolated
estimates also showed higher variance than the corresponding raw
estimates, indicating that the method improved the mean estimate but did
not stabilize trial-level output.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Recent studies have explored the use of variational quantum models for
classification tasks, including applications related to Natural Language
Processing (NLP). However, current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ)
devices remain affected by decoherence, gate imperfections, crosstalk, and
readout or measurement noise, which can make quantum model outputs unstable in
practical settings \cite{b_njiki2026, b_koenig2024}.

For small variational quantum classifiers, noise is especially important because
the measured output depends on repeated circuit executions. Finite measurement
shots can introduce sampling variation, while depolarizing noise can alter the
observed bitstring distribution. These effects make it difficult to interpret a
single noisy evaluation as a stable estimate of classifier performance. Because
of this, repeated simulation trials are useful for measuring how much the
classifier output varies under the same experimental configuration.

Error mitigation methods such as Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) are commonly
used to estimate a circuit output closer to the zero-noise case by evaluating
the same circuit under increased noise levels and extrapolating the measured
values back toward the zero-noise limit \cite{b_koenig2024, b_mohammadipour2025}. 
In this study, ZNE is used only as a
post-processing method for noisy evaluation. The VQC is trained once under a
clean setting, and the trained parameters are reused during noisy and
ZNE-based simulations.

This study uses a Monte Carlo simulation framework to examine how simulated
depolarizing noise, measurement shot count, and Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)
affect the behavior of a fixed two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)
for binary intent classification. The pipeline evaluates 1{,}000 seed-varied
Monte Carlo trials for each configuration of depolarizing noise at 0\%, 1\%,
3\%, 5\%, 7\%, and 10\%, combined with measurement shot counts of 100,
1{,}000, and 10{,}000. This repeated evaluation is used to estimate the mean
accuracy and variance introduced by finite-shot sampling and simulated
depolarizing noise.

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:related_literature}

\subsection{Quantum Models for NLP and Classification}

Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP) studies how quantum circuits and quantum-inspired
mathematical structures can be applied to language-related tasks. Much of the early motivation
comes from compositional models of meaning, especially the Distributional Compositional
Categorical (DisCoCat) framework, where grammar and vector-space meaning are treated within
one formal structure \cite{b_coeckesadrzadeh2010, b_coecke2020, b_guarasci2022}. Later studies
extended this direction toward quantum circuit implementations and small-scale NLP experiments
on Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) hardware \cite{b_lorenz2023, b_phukan2025}.

This study is related to that broader QNLP direction, but it does not implement a full
grammar-to-circuit DisCoCat pipeline. Instead, it treats intent classification as a small binary
classification task. Text utterances are first represented using TF-IDF, reduced to two numerical
features through PCA, and then evaluated using a two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC).
Because of this design, the work is closer to a controlled quantum machine learning classification
experiment than to a full compositional language model.

\subsection{DisCoCat and Quantum Circuit Motivation}

The DisCoCat framework is useful as background because it explains why quantum methods are
often discussed together with language structure. In DisCoCat, grammatical structure determines
how word meanings are composed, and this composition can be represented using tensor-based or
diagrammatic structures \cite{b_coeckesadrzadeh2010, b_zengcoecke2016}. Related work has also
shown how these diagrammatic representations can be reformulated or extended through different
grammar formalisms and higher-order models \cite{b_yeung2021, b_toumi2025}. More recent work
has considered how larger text structures may be converted into parameterized quantum circuits,
although such approaches require more complex parsing and circuit-generation pipelines than the
one used in this paper \cite{b_krawchuk2025}.

For the present experiment, these studies are used mainly to situate the work within QNLP and
quantum machine learning. The implemented pipeline does not parse sentence grammar or assign
quantum circuits to syntactic structures. The language component is limited to feature extraction
from short text utterances before quantum classification.

\subsection{Small Variational Quantum Classifiers}

Variational Quantum Classifiers use parameterized quantum circuits for supervised learning.
They are usually built from three parts: a feature map that encodes classical data, an ansatz with
trainable parameters, and a measurement rule that maps circuit outputs to class labels. Since
near-term quantum models are limited by qubit count and circuit depth, dimensionality reduction
is commonly needed when classical input data has more features than the available number of
qubits.

Related VQC studies show that PCA and other reduction methods are often used to map classical
features into a smaller latent space before quantum encoding \cite{b_higgs}. Other benchmark
studies on low-dimensional classification tasks show that two-qubit or shallow VQC models can
be useful for controlled experiments, even when they do not yet show a clear advantage over
classical models \cite{b_seilkhan2026}. In QNLP-oriented studies, ansatz choice is also important
because the circuit structure affects trainability and classification behavior \cite{b_khatri2022}.
These points support the design choice of using a two-dimensional PCA representation and a
two-qubit VQC in this study.

\subsection{Noise, Trainability, and Measurement Variation}

Noise remains a central limitation of near-term quantum models. In variational quantum
algorithms, hardware noise and finite measurement shots can affect the measured output of the
circuit, which can make a single evaluation unstable. Noise can also interact with trainability.
Recent results suggest that noise effects are not uniformly harmful: in some quantum machine
learning settings, moderate noise can behave like an implicit regularizer for under-optimized
models, while better-converged models may degrade under the same noise conditions
\cite{b_zhu2026}. At the same time, other work shows that noise can flatten variational cost
landscapes and reduce trainability, especially in noise-induced barren plateau regimes
\cite{b_wang2021}.

Barren plateaus and noise-related trainability problems are also relevant to VQC research because
they can make optimization difficult, especially as circuit width, circuit depth, or noise exposure
increases \cite{b_lung2026, b_wang2021}. Other studies on hybrid quantum neural networks show that the
effect of error mitigation depends on the noise model, the circuit, and the training or evaluation
setting \cite{b_njiki2026}. These works motivate the need to evaluate not only the mean accuracy
of a noisy VQC, but also the variance of its output under repeated simulation trials.

\subsection{Zero-Noise Extrapolation}

Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) is an error mitigation method that estimates a zero-noise output
by evaluating a circuit at increased noise levels and extrapolating the resulting measurements
back toward the zero-noise limit. Several ZNE approaches have been proposed, including methods
based on circuit folding, inverted circuits, layout permutations, and polynomial or regression-based
extrapolation \cite{b_koenig2024, b_sayapin2025, b_mohammadipour2025}.

This paper uses a simpler controlled simulation setting. Instead of comparing different ZNE
protocols, the experiment scales a simulated depolarizing noise level using factors of 1, 3, and 5.
A linear fit is then used to estimate the intercept at zero noise. This choice keeps the experiment
focused on how ZNE changes the measured accuracy and variance of a fixed two-qubit VQC under
repeated Monte Carlo trials.

\subsection{Synthesis and Research Gap}

The reviewed literature shows three relevant directions. First, QNLP provides the broader
motivation for testing quantum models on language-related tasks. Second, small VQC studies show
how low-dimensional classical data can be encoded into shallow quantum classifiers. Third, work
on noise and ZNE shows that error mitigation can improve estimates in some settings, but may also
increase variance or behave differently across noise models.

Based on this, the gap addressed in this paper is narrow and implementation-specific: there is
limited evaluation of how simulated depolarizing noise, measurement shot count, and linear ZNE
affect the accuracy and variance of a fixed two-qubit VQC for binary intent classification. This
study addresses that gap through a Monte Carlo simulation design using repeated seed-varied
evaluations across multiple noise-shot configurations.

\section{Methodology}

This study uses a controlled simulation-based methodology to evaluate how
depolarizing noise, measurement shot count, and Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)
affect the accuracy and variance of a two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier
(VQC). The workflow follows a fixed-model evaluation design. The VQC is first
trained under a clean statevector setting. After training, the learned ansatz
parameters are extracted and reused during noisy and ZNE-based simulations. The
VQC is not retrained during noisy evaluation. This isolates the effects of
simulator noise, measurement shots, and ZNE from variation caused by repeated
model training.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation and Feature Extraction}

The experiment uses the Tanaos synthetic intent classifier dataset
\cite{b_tanaos_dataset}, a Hugging Face-hosted English text classification
dataset with numeric intent labels from 0 to 11. This study restricts the task
to two intent categories: Greeting and Farewell, corresponding to labels 0 and 1. The
selected subset is balanced by sampling 500 utterances from each class, producing
1{,}000 total samples. The balanced dataset is divided into training and testing
sets using an 80/20 stratified split, producing 800 training samples and 200
testing samples.

Text features are extracted using Term Frequency--Inverse Document Frequency
(TF-IDF) with English stopword removal and a maximum vocabulary size of 500.
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is then applied to reduce the TF-IDF feature
space to two components, matching the two-qubit input dimension of the VQC. The
TF-IDF vectorizer and PCA transformation are fitted only on the training set and
then applied to the test set to avoid information leakage.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Dataset Preparation and Feature Extraction}
\label{alg:data_features}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Dataset $D$, target labels $L=\{0,1\}$, samples per label $n=500$, test ratio $r=0.20$, base seed $s_0=42$
\Ensure Training features $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, test features $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, labels $y_{\mathrm{train}}$, $y_{\mathrm{test}}$
\State Load the Tanaos synthetic intent classifier dataset.
\State Standardize the label column name to \texttt{label}, if needed.
\State Filter $D$ to retain only \textit{Greeting} and \textit{Farewell}.
\For{each label $\ell \in L$}
    \State Randomly sample $n$ examples from class $\ell$ using seed $s_0$.
\EndFor
\State Concatenate the sampled class subsets into a balanced dataset $D_b$.
\State Split $D_b$ into train and test sets using stratified sampling with test ratio $r$ and seed $s_0$.
\State Fit a TF-IDF vectorizer on the training text with English stopword removal and a 500-feature limit.
\State Transform the training and test text into TF-IDF matrices.
\State Fit PCA with two components on the training TF-IDF matrix.
\State Transform both training and test TF-IDF matrices using the fitted PCA model.
\State \Return $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, $y_{\mathrm{train}}$, $y_{\mathrm{test}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Variational Quantum Classifier Training}

The VQC uses a \texttt{ZZFeatureMap} with one repetition for data encoding and a
\texttt{RealAmplitudes} ansatz with two repetitions for trainable quantum
parameters. This produces six trainable ansatz parameters. The model is trained
using the COBYLA optimizer with a maximum of 100 iterations. Clean training uses Qiskit's 
\texttt{StatevectorSampler}; therefore, the training stage does not
use the Aer depolarizing-noise model or the noisy-evaluation shot-count sweep.

The classifier uses a binary parity interpretation. Even-parity measured
bitstrings are mapped to class 0, while odd-parity bitstrings are mapped to class
1. The same parity rule is also used during noisy simulation so that clean and
noisy evaluation follow the same label-mapping rule.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Clean VQC Training}
\label{alg:vqc_training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Training features $X_{\mathrm{train}}$, training labels $y_{\mathrm{train}}$
\Ensure Trained VQC model $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$
\State Construct a two-qubit \texttt{ZZFeatureMap} $U_{\phi}$ with one repetition.
\State Construct a two-qubit \texttt{RealAmplitudes} ansatz $U_{\theta}$ with two repetitions.
\State Define the parity interpretation $f(b)$.
\State Map even-parity bitstrings to class 0.
\State Map odd-parity bitstrings to class 1.
\State Initialize a VQC using $U_{\phi}$, $U_{\theta}$, COBYLA, \texttt{StatevectorSampler}, and parity output interpretation.
\State Train the VQC on $(X_{\mathrm{train}}, y_{\mathrm{train}})$.
\State \Return trained model $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Clean Baseline Evaluation}

After training, the clean VQC is evaluated on the held-out test set. This
baseline evaluation does not use simulated depolarizing noise or ZNE. Accuracy,
a classification report, and a confusion matrix are generated. The clean
baseline is used as a reference for checking whether the fixed noisy evaluator
remains consistent with the trained classifier when the base noise level is zero.

\subsection{Fixed-Model Noisy Evaluation}

Noisy evaluation is performed using Qiskit Aer with an optional depolarizing
noise model. For a noise level of 0.00, the simulator is run without a noise
model. For nonzero noise levels, single-qubit depolarizing errors are attached
to common one-qubit gates, and two-qubit depolarizing errors are attached to
common two-qubit gates.

The noisy evaluator uses the trained VQC as a fixed model. The trained ansatz
weights are extracted from the fitted VQC and reused for all noisy simulations.
The feature map and ansatz are composed into a single parameterized circuit,
measurement operations are added, and the circuit is decomposed before being
passed through a preset transpilation pass manager. The pass manager and Aer
backend are both seeded using the Monte Carlo trial seed. After transpilation,
each test sample is bound to the feature-map parameters, while the same trained
ansatz weights are bound to the ansatz parameters for every sample. The bound
test circuits are executed as a batch using the selected shot count. Measurement
counts are converted into class predictions using the same parity rule used by
the clean VQC.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Fixed-Model Noisy VQC Evaluation}
\label{alg:noisy_eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained VQC $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$, test features $X_{\mathrm{test}}$, test labels $y_{\mathrm{test}}$, noise level $\lambda$, shots $q$, seed $s$
\Ensure Noisy accuracy $A(\lambda,q,s)$

\State Extract trained ansatz weights $\hat{\theta}$ from $M$.
\State Compose the parameterized circuit $C$ by applying $U_{\phi}$ followed by $U_{\theta}$.
\State Add measurement operations to all qubits in $C$.
\State Decompose $C$ into simulator-compatible basis operations.

\If{$\lambda \leq 0$}
    \State Set the noise model $N \gets \texttt{None}$.
\Else
    \State Build a depolarizing noise model $N$ with error rate $\lambda$.
    \State Attach one-qubit depolarizing errors to common one-qubit gates.
    \State Attach two-qubit depolarizing errors to common two-qubit gates.
\EndIf

\State Initialize an Aer simulator backend using noise model $N$ and simulator seed $s$.
\State Generate a preset pass manager with optimization level 1, backend information, and transpiler seed $s$.
\State Transpile the decomposed parameterized circuit $C$ using the pass manager, producing $C'$.

\State Initialize an empty list of bound circuits $B$.
\For{each test sample $x_i \in X_{\mathrm{test}}$}
    \State Create a parameter assignment dictionary.
    \State Bind the components of $x_i$ to the feature-map parameters of $U_{\phi}$.
    \State Bind the trained weights $\hat{\theta}$ to the ansatz parameters of $U_{\theta}$.
    \State Assign all parameters to the transpiled circuit $C'$ to produce a bound circuit $C_i$.
    \State Append $C_i$ to $B$.
\EndFor

\State Execute all bound circuits $B$ as one batch on the Aer backend using $q$ shots.
\State Initialize an empty prediction list $\hat{Y}$.

\For{each circuit result index $i$}
    \State Retrieve the measured bitstring counts for bound circuit $C_i$.
    \State Remove spaces from each measured bitstring, if present.
    \State Compute the parity of each bitstring.
    \State Accumulate even-parity counts as class 0 and odd-parity counts as class 1.
    \If{class 0 count is greater than or equal to class 1 count}
        \State Predict class 0.
    \Else
        \State Predict class 1.
    \EndIf
    \State Append the predicted class to $\hat{Y}$.
\EndFor

\State Compute $A(\lambda,q,s)$ as the accuracy between $y_{\mathrm{test}}$ and $\hat{Y}$.
\State \Return $A(\lambda,q,s)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Noise and Shot-Count Sweep}

The experiment evaluates six discrete base depolarizing noise levels:

\[
0\%,\ 1\%,\ 3\%,\ 5\%,\ 7\%,\ 10\%.
\]

For each base noise level, the fixed VQC is evaluated under three measurement
shot counts:

\[
100,\ 1{,}000,\ 10{,}000.
\]

This produces 18 noise-shot configurations. Since the VQC is not retrained
across these configurations, changes in performance are attributed to simulated
noise level, measurement shot count, and Monte Carlo sampling seed.

\subsection{Zero-Noise Extrapolation}

Zero-Noise Extrapolation is applied as a post-processing error mitigation
procedure. In general, ZNE estimates a zero-noise value by evaluating a circuit
at multiple amplified noise levels and fitting or extrapolating the measured
outputs back to the zero-noise limit \cite{b_koenig2024, b_mohammadipour2025}.
For each base noise level $\lambda$, the trained classifier is evaluated at
three noise scale factors:

\[
1,\ 3,\ 5.
\]

The scaled noise levels are therefore

\[
\lambda,\ 3\lambda,\ 5\lambda.
\]

The accuracy measured at scale factor 1 is treated as the raw noisy accuracy.
The ZNE estimate is obtained by fitting a linear model between scaled noise
levels and observed accuracies. The fitted intercept at zero noise is used as
the ZNE-mitigated accuracy estimate. If all scaled noise levels are identical,
as in the 0.00 base-noise case, the measured accuracy is returned directly. The
extrapolated accuracy is clipped to the interval $[0,1]$.

In this implementation, extrapolation is applied to the aggregate
classification accuracy rather than to each circuit expectation value or
sample-level class probability. Therefore, the procedure is best understood
as an accuracy-level ZNE-style estimate. This design is useful for studying
how the measured classifier accuracy changes under controlled noise
scaling, but it should not be interpreted as a full observable-level ZNE
prediction pipeline.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Zero-Noise Extrapolation for One Configuration}
\label{alg:zne}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained VQC $M$, feature map $U_{\phi}$, ansatz $U_{\theta}$, test set $(X_{\mathrm{test}}, y_{\mathrm{test}})$, base noise $\lambda$, shots $q$, seed $s$, scale factors $S=\{1,3,5\}$
\Ensure Raw accuracy $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, ZNE accuracy $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, ZNE delta $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$
\State Initialize empty lists $N \gets [\ ]$ and $A \gets [\ ]$.
\For{each scale factor $k \in S$}
    \State Compute scaled noise $\lambda_k \gets \min(k\lambda, 1.0)$.
    \State Evaluate the fixed VQC under $\lambda_k$ using Algorithm~\ref{alg:noisy_eval}.
    \State Append $\lambda_k$ to $N$ and the measured accuracy $A_k$ to $A$.
\EndFor
\State Set $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$ to the accuracy measured at scale factor 1.
\If{all values in $N$ are identical}
    \State Set $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets A_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
\Else
    \State Fit a linear model $A_k = m\lambda_k + b$ over the pairs $(N,A)$.
    \State Set $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets b$.
    \State Clip $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ to the interval $[0,1]$.
\EndIf
\State Compute $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}} \gets A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - A_{\mathrm{raw}}$.
\State \Return $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Monte Carlo Evaluation Design}

A base random seed of 42 is used for reproducibility. This seed controls dataset
sampling, the train-test split, and the initial Python and NumPy random states.
For the Monte Carlo sweep, the simulator seed is not kept constant. For each
trial, the seed is computed as

\[
s_t = s_0 + t,
\]

where $s_0=42$ and $t$ is the trial index. Thus, each noise-shot configuration
is evaluated across 1{,}000 deterministic but distinct simulator seeds. The same
seed sequence is reused across different noise-shot configurations, keeping
comparisons reproducible and paired by trial index.

Because there are six base noise levels, three shot counts, and 1{,}000 trials
per configuration, the complete sweep contains

\[
6 \times 3 \times 1{,}000 = 18{,}000
\]

ZNE evaluation jobs. Each ZNE job evaluates three scaled noise levels, so the
full sweep performs

\[
18{,}000 \times 3 = 54{,}000
\]

scaled noisy evaluations.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo ZNE Sweep}
\label{alg:mc_sweep}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Noise levels $\Lambda=\{0.00,0.01,0.03,0.05,0.07,0.10\}$, shot counts $Q=\{100,1000,10000\}$, trials $T=1000$, base seed $s_0=42$, scale factors $S=\{1,3,5\}$
\Ensure Raw trial table $R$
\State Initialize empty result table $R$.
\For{each base noise level $\lambda \in \Lambda$}
    \For{each shot count $q \in Q$}
        \For{$t = 0$ to $T-1$}
            \State Set trial seed $s_t \gets s_0 + t$.
            \State Run Algorithm~\ref{alg:zne} using $\lambda$, $q$, $s_t$, and $S$.
            \State Store $\lambda$, $q$, $s_t$, scaled noise levels, scaled accuracies, $A_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$, and $\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ in $R$.
        \EndFor
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State Sort $R$ by base noise level, shot count, and seed.
\State \Return $R$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo Result Summarization and Output Generation}
\label{alg:mc_postprocessing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Raw trial table $R$
\Ensure Grouped summary table $G$, saved CSV files, and result figures
\State Group $R$ by base noise level and shot count.
\State Compute mean raw accuracy for each group.
\State Compute mean ZNE accuracy for each group.
\State Compute raw and ZNE accuracy variance for each group.
\State Compute raw and ZNE accuracy standard deviation for each group.
\State Compute mean ZNE delta for each group.
\State Compute minimum and maximum ZNE accuracy for each group.
\State Count the number of Monte Carlo trials in each group.
\State Store the grouped statistics in summary table $G$.
\State Generate the raw-versus-ZNE mean accuracy plot.
\State Generate the raw-versus-ZNE accuracy variance plot.
\State Generate the ZNE accuracy gain plot.
\State Save $R$ and $G$ as timestamped CSV files.
\State \Return $G$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The Monte Carlo sweep produces the raw trial-level ZNE results. These raw results
are then converted into a dataframe, grouped by base noise level and shot count,
summarized statistically, plotted, and saved as timestamped CSV files in separate
post-processing steps.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The primary metric is classification accuracy on the held-out test set. For each
noise-shot configuration, the 1{,}000 Monte Carlo trials are summarized using
mean raw accuracy, mean ZNE accuracy, raw accuracy variance, ZNE accuracy
variance, raw standard deviation, ZNE standard deviation, mean ZNE delta, minimum
ZNE accuracy, and maximum ZNE accuracy.

The ZNE accuracy delta is defined as

\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{ZNE}} = A_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - A_{\mathrm{raw}},
\]

where $A_{\mathrm{ZNE}}$ is the extrapolated ZNE accuracy and
$A_{\mathrm{raw}}$ is the accuracy measured at the unscaled base noise level.
The raw Monte Carlo outputs and grouped summary statistics are saved as
timestamped CSV files to prevent accidental overwriting of previous experiment
outputs.

\section{Results}

The experiment evaluated six base depolarizing noise levels, three shot counts,
and 1{,}000 Monte Carlo trials per noise-shot configuration, resulting in
18{,}000 ZNE jobs. Each ZNE job evaluates the fixed VQC at three scaled noise
levels, giving 54{,}000 scaled noisy evaluation calls.

\subsection{Clean Baseline Performance}

in the clean baseline setting. Because the result depends on the sampled dataset
split, optimizer behavior, and software execution environment, small differences

\subsection{Effect of Shot Count on Stability}

Increasing the shot count reduced the Monte Carlo spread of the raw and ZNE
accuracy estimates. Averaged across all noise levels, the raw standard deviation
was approximately 2.85 percentage points at 100 shots, 2.11 percentage points at
1{,}000 shots, and 1.02 percentage points at 10{,}000 shots. The same pattern
appeared for ZNE, although ZNE remained more variable than the corresponding raw
estimate.

amplify trial-level variation.

Figure~\ref{fig:zne_accuracy_gain} shows the mean ZNE accuracy gain for each
noise-shot configuration. The gain was small at 0.01 noise, became more visible
at moderate noise levels, and remained positive at 0.10 noise. The strongest
gain occurred at 0.07 noise with 10{,}000 shots.

These results indicate that ZNE improved the mean accuracy of the fixed trained
VQC under simulated depolarizing noise, but the improvement came with increased

two-qubit simulation, linear ZNE can improve average noisy evaluation accuracy,
but it does not uniformly stabilize the classifier output. The method is better
understood as an accuracy-bias correction technique rather than a general
variance-reduction technique.

\subsection{Limitations}

This experiment has several limitations. First, the classifier uses only two
intent classes and a two-dimensional PCA representation, so the results do
not directly generalize to larger intent-classification settings. This design is
consistent with small VQC studies that reduce classical features before quantum
encoding, but it remains a constrained low-dimensional setting rather than a
large-scale intent classification model \cite{b_higgs, b_seilkhan2026}. Second,
the VQC is evaluated only under a simulated depolarizing noise model. Real
NISQ hardware can include additional error sources such as readout error,
relaxation, dephasing, crosstalk, and device-specific gate variation, which may
change the behavior of a quantum classifier and its mitigation results
\cite{b_njiki2026, b_koenig2024}. Third, the ZNE procedure in this study is
applied to aggregate classification accuracy rather than directly to expectation
values or class probabilities. Standard ZNE methods are usually described in
terms of extrapolating measured circuit outputs or observable expectation values
toward the zero-noise limit \cite{b_koenig2024, b_mohammadipour2025}. Finally,
the study does not claim quantum advantage over classical classifiers. Prior
QNLP and VQC studies similarly note that near-term experiments are mainly
proof-of-concept or controlled benchmark studies rather than demonstrations of
practical quantum advantage \cite{b_lorenz2023, b_seilkhan2026}.


\section{Conclusion}

This study evaluated a fixed two-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier
for binary intent classification under simulated depolarizing noise and
finite-shot measurement conditions. The clean statevector model reached
an accuracy of ~60.00\% on the held-out test set. Across the nonzero-noise
configurations, the linear zero-noise extrapolated accuracy estimate was
higher than the raw noisy accuracy by an average of approximately 1.45
percentage points, with the largest gain of 3.67 percentage points
observed at 0.07 depolarizing noise and 10,000 shots.

The results suggest that linear noise extrapolation can improve the mean
accuracy estimate of this fixed two-qubit classifier under the tested
simulation conditions. However, the extrapolated estimates also showed
higher variance than the corresponding raw noisy estimates. Therefore,
the method should be interpreted as a bias-correction approach rather
than a variance-reduction method.

The findings are limited to a small two-class dataset, a two-qubit VQC,
a simulated depolarizing noise model, and accuracy-level extrapolation.
Future work should evaluate larger intent sets, compare against classical
baselines using the same PCA features, test additional quantum ansätze,
and apply ZNE directly to circuit expectation values or class probabilities
before computing classification accuracy.
