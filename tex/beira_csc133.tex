% -------------------------------------------------------
% Abstract
% -------------------------------------------------------
\def\myabstract{%
  \mdseries
This study presents a Monte Carlo simulation framework to evaluate the
performance of a \textbf{2-qubit Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)} for binary
intent classification under Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ)
conditions. Using a focused subset of the 
\href{https://huggingface.co/datasets/tanaos/synthetic-intent-classifier-dataset-v1}{\textbf{Tanaos synthetic intent classifier v1}} dataset, 
the task is restricted to \textbf{``Greeting''} and \textbf{``Farewell''} categories to
suit the 2-qubit architecture. Text utterances are vectorized via TF-IDF and
reduced to a 2-dimensional feature space using Principal Component Analysis (PCA). 
The model architecture utilizes a ZZFeatureMap for quantum state
encoding and a RealAmplitudes variational circuit with six trainable
parameters. To combat hardware imperfections, we integrate \textbf{Zero Noise Extrapolation (ZNE)} 
as an error mitigation strategy. We systematically evaluate the pipeline by varying 
depolarizing noise levels (0--10\%), measurement shot counts (100--10{,}000), and circuit 
repetitions. Through 1{,}000 independent Monte Carlo trials per configuration, this 
research reports the mean accuracy and statistical variance, demonstrating the efficacy 
of ZNE in establishing a noise-resilient reliability baseline for near-term quantum NLU 
tasks compared against a classical linear baseline.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
As the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) explores the
potential of quantum-enhanced machine learning,
a primary bottleneck remains the susceptibility of Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) 
devices to environmental decoherence and gate errors. \cite{b_phukan2025}.
This research employs Monte Carlo simulations to analyze the impact of increasing noise and
variable measurement precision on classification accuracy within a constrained quantum circuit.

NISQ-era quantum computers are constrained by two primary technical limitations: 
the rapid onset of environmental decoherence and high gate error rates, 
which typically range from 0.1\% to 1\%. 
Unlike classical systems, quantum noise compounds dynamically with every physical operation,
causing the fidelity of the overall quantum state to decay as circuit depth and gate counts scale. 
This operational decay frequently restricts reliable computation to shallow architectures of fewer than 100 layers \cite{b_zhu2026}.
Without error mitigation, current hardware cannot scale beyond these architectural limits.

Optimizing high-dimensional Natural Language Understanding (NLU) models often
introduces the additional challenge of the 'barren plateau' phenomenon. 
Here, cost function gradients vanish exponentially as 
the qubit count scales, amplifying the underlying hardware instability \cite{b_phukan2025}. 
These plateaus effectively trap optimizers in suboptimal search regions, often leading to premature 
convergence and poor classification performance \cite{b_lung2026}

Analyses of quantum noise dynamics reveal that controlled stochasticity acts as an
implicit regularizer by inducing an exponential contraction of the model’s Lipschitz constant 
and a shrinkage of the Quantum Fisher Information Matrix (QFIM) \cite{b_zhu2026} \cite{b_xhelollari}.
These mechanisms yield flatter minima in the loss landscape, which have been shown to yield tighter
Probably Approximately Correct (PAC)-Bayesian generalization bounds \cite{b_xhelollari}.
However, this benefit operates within a precarious 'regularization vs. trainability trade-off' \cite{b_zhu2026} \cite{b_cincio2021}.
Beyond a critical tipping point, the same state-space contraction triggers noise-induced barren plateaus (NIBPs), 
where gradients vanish exponentially and stochastic fluctuations transition 
from beneficial explorers to destructive forces \cite{b_xhelollari} \cite{b_higgs}.

The utility of the noise depends strictly on model initialization: it assists under-optimized models 
but disrupts well-converged ones, establishing a negative correlation with baseline performance \cite{b_zhu2026}.
A system-wide reliability boundary provides a framework to evaluate where hardware-induced 
decoherence begins to overwhelm predictive utility. This shift typically manifests as noise 
introduces granularity and local irregularities into the continuous decision function. \cite{b_seilkhan2026}

A Monte Carlo simulation framework serves to isolate the transition between beneficial regularization and 
catastrophic decoherence. This proposed pipeline evaluates 1,000 independent trials for each discrete 
configuration of depolarizing noise (1–10\%) and measurement shot counts.

High-volume sampling is utilized here to quantitatively capture the statistical variance introduced by hardware-induced fluctuations 
and finite-shot sampling noise, both of which risk distorting gradient estimates and model outputs \cite{{b_seilkhan2026}}.
